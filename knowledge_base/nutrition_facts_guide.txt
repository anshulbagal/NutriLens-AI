NUTRITION FACTS LABEL GUIDE - REFERENCE DOCUMENT
==================================================

SERVING SIZE AND SERVINGS PER CONTAINER
- Serving size is the amount of food customarily eaten at one time.
- All nutrient amounts on the label are based on one serving.
- If you eat more than one serving, multiply all values accordingly.
- "Servings per container" tells you how many servings are in the whole package.

CALORIES
- Calories measure how much energy you get from a serving of food.
- General daily calorie intake guidance: ~2000 calories/day for adults.
- 40 calories per serving = Low
- 100 calories per serving = Moderate
- 400+ calories per serving = High
- Calories from fat are no longer required on modern labels but saturated/trans fat still must be listed.

MACRONUTRIENTS

Total Fat:
- Includes saturated fat, trans fat, polyunsaturated fat, and monounsaturated fat.
- Fat is essential for energy and absorbing vitamins A, D, E, K.
- %DV: 5% or less is low; 20% or more is high.
- Recommended daily intake: less than 65g based on 2000 calorie diet.

Saturated Fat:
- Found in animal products: butter, cheese, meat, full-fat dairy.
- Excess saturated fat raises LDL (bad) cholesterol, increasing cardiovascular risk.
- Daily limit: less than 20g (less than 10% of calories).
- %DV: 5% or less is low; 20% or more is high.
- American Heart Association recommends <7% of total calories from saturated fat.

Trans Fat:
- Created by partial hydrogenation of vegetable oils (partially hydrogenated oils).
- Raises LDL cholesterol AND lowers HDL (good) cholesterol — worse than saturated fat.
- FDA banned partially hydrogenated oils (PHOs) in 2018.
- Even "0g trans fat" can contain up to 0.5g per serving by law.
- Avoid any product listing "partially hydrogenated" in ingredients.

Cholesterol:
- Daily limit: less than 300mg.
- For those with cardiovascular disease: less than 200mg/day.
- Found in animal products: eggs, meat, shellfish, dairy.
- Plant foods contain zero cholesterol.

Sodium:
- Daily limit: less than 2300mg (about 1 teaspoon of salt).
- For hypertensive individuals: less than 1500mg.
- Low sodium: 140mg or less per serving.
- Very low sodium: 35mg or less per serving.
- Sodium-free: less than 5mg per serving.
- Hidden sodium sources: bread, canned soups, processed meats, condiments, cheese.

Total Carbohydrates:
- Includes dietary fiber, total sugars, and added sugars.
- Recommended: 225–325g/day based on 2000 calorie diet.
- Carbs provide 4 calories per gram.

Dietary Fiber:
- Daily recommended intake: 25g (women), 38g (men).
- Soluble fiber (oats, beans, apples): lowers LDL cholesterol, slows glucose absorption.
- Insoluble fiber (whole wheat, vegetables): improves gut motility, prevents constipation.
- Good source of fiber: 2.5g or more per serving.
- Excellent source of fiber: 5g or more per serving.

Total Sugars:
- Includes naturally occurring sugars (from fruit, dairy) and added sugars.
- Does not distinguish between natural and added on its own — check "Added Sugars" line.

Added Sugars:
- Sugars and syrups added during processing.
- Daily limit: less than 50g (10% of total calories).
- American Heart Association: women <25g/day, men <36g/day.
- Common names for added sugars: sucrose, high-fructose corn syrup, dextrose, maltose, corn syrup, honey, agave nectar, maple syrup, molasses, fruit juice concentrate, cane sugar, evaporated cane juice.

Protein:
- Essential for building and repairing tissues, enzymes, hormones, immune function.
- Provides 4 calories per gram.
- Recommended intake: 0.8g per kg of body weight for sedentary adults; 1.2–2.0g/kg for athletes.
- Complete proteins (all essential amino acids): meat, fish, eggs, dairy, soy, quinoa.
- Incomplete proteins: most plant proteins — combine them (rice + beans) for complete profile.

VITAMINS AND MINERALS

Vitamin D:
- Required for calcium absorption and bone health; immune function.
- Daily Value: 20 mcg (800 IU).
- Deficiency linked to osteoporosis, depression, weakened immunity.
- Food sources: fatty fish, fortified milk, egg yolks, sunlight exposure.

Calcium:
- Essential for bone and teeth strength, nerve function, muscle contraction.
- Daily Value: 1300mg.
- Good sources: dairy, fortified plant milks, leafy greens (kale, bok choy), tofu, sardines.
- Calcium absorption requires Vitamin D.

Iron:
- Essential for hemoglobin production, oxygen transport.
- Daily Value: 18mg.
- Heme iron (animal sources) absorbed 2–3x better than non-heme iron (plant sources).
- Good sources: red meat, poultry, shellfish, beans, lentils, tofu, fortified cereals.
- Vitamin C enhances non-heme iron absorption.
- Deficiency leads to anemia: fatigue, weakness, pale skin.

Potassium:
- Regulates fluid balance, nerve signals, blood pressure.
- Daily Value: 4700mg.
- Low potassium diet associated with higher blood pressure.
- Good sources: bananas, potatoes, avocados, spinach, beans, dairy.

PERCENT DAILY VALUE (%DV)
- %DV tells you how much a nutrient in a serving contributes to a daily diet.
- Based on 2,000 calorie daily diet.
- 5% DV or less = Low (for nutrients you want less of: saturated fat, sodium, added sugars)
- 20% DV or more = High (good for nutrients you want more of: fiber, vitamins, minerals)
- Use %DV to compare foods and make healthier choices.

HEALTH CLAIMS AND LABEL TERMS
- "Low fat": 3g or less of fat per serving.
- "Fat free": less than 0.5g fat per serving.
- "Low calorie": 40 calories or less per serving.
- "Calorie free": less than 5 calories per serving.
- "Low sodium": 140mg or less per serving.
- "Reduced": at least 25% less of a nutrient compared to regular version.
- "Light" or "Lite": 1/3 fewer calories or 50% less fat than reference food.
- "High", "Rich in", "Excellent source of": 20% DV or more.
- "Good source of": 10–19% DV.
- "Fortified", "Enriched", "Added": 10% more of a nutrient than reference food.
- "Natural": no added colors, artificial flavors, or synthetic substances — not the same as "organic".
- "Organic": certified by USDA, grown without synthetic pesticides or fertilizers.

GLYCEMIC INDEX AND GLYCEMIC LOAD
- Glycemic Index (GI): ranks carbohydrate foods by how quickly they raise blood glucose.
  - Low GI: ≤55 (lentils, oats, most fruits and vegetables)
  - Medium GI: 56–69 (whole wheat bread, brown rice, sweet corn)
  - High GI: ≥70 (white bread, white rice, most cereals, potatoes)
- Glycemic Load (GL) = GI x carb content / 100 — accounts for portion size.
- High GI foods cause rapid blood sugar spikes and crashes; linked to diabetes risk.

OMEGA FATTY ACIDS
- Omega-3 (ALA, EPA, DHA): anti-inflammatory, cardioprotective, brain health.
  - Sources: fatty fish (salmon, mackerel, sardines), flaxseed, chia, walnuts.
  - Recommended: 1.1–1.6g/day ALA; EPA/DHA from fatty fish 2x/week.
- Omega-6 (linoleic acid): necessary but excessive intake (Western diet) promotes inflammation.
  - Sources: vegetable oils (sunflower, corn, soybean), nuts.
- Omega-9 (oleic acid): monounsaturated fat, heart-healthy.
  - Sources: olive oil, avocado, almonds.
- Optimal omega-6 to omega-3 ratio: 4:1 or less (Western diet often 15:1 to 20:1).

SUGAR ALCOHOLS (POLYOLS)
- Erythritol, xylitol, sorbitol, mannitol, maltitol, lactitol, isomalt.
- Provide fewer calories than regular sugar (0.2–3 cal/g vs 4 cal/g).
- Don't raise blood sugar significantly (low GI).
- Excessive intake (>20g sorbitol, >50g mannitol) can cause bloating, gas, diarrhea.
- Erythritol has least GI impact and best tolerance.

ARTIFICIAL SWEETENERS
- Aspartame: 200x sweeter than sugar; controversial but FDA-approved; avoid in PKU (phenylketonuria).
- Sucralose (Splenda): 600x sweeter; stable at high temperatures; FDA-approved.
- Saccharin: 300x sweeter; oldest artificial sweetener; FDA-approved.
- Stevia (Rebaudioside A): plant-derived; FDA "generally recognized as safe" (GRAS).
- Acesulfame-K: 200x sweeter; often combined with aspartame; FDA-approved.
- None have proven long-term harm at recommended levels but some studies suggest microbiome effects.
